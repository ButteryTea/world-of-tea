History of Tea

The origins of tea and its reformations, influence, and signifiance worldwide.

Origins of China

Tea was believed to originate in China 6,000 years ago when an ancient emperor called Shen Nung discovered the existence and medicinal properties of tea after searching for edible grains and herbs and poisoning himself 72 times in the process.

After realizing the medicinal benefits of tea to a person's health, the emperor then urged the Chinese people to cultivate and consume the plant and its leaves - making tea a staple and traditional ingredient in the food and drink of the Chinese culture.

Discovery of Tea as a Drink

Tea was originally used as an ingredient for medicinal herbs and seasoning for different dishes. It only shifted from food to drink 1,500 years ago in China when the people realized that a combination of heat and moisture could create a variety of complex flavors from the plant.

Throughout the years and after many variations of preparations for handling tea, the Chinese standardized heating tea by packing it into portable circular cakes (broken up and ground into powder) or loose-leaf teas and mixing it with water to make a tea beverage.

Introduction to Japan

During the 8th century of the Tang Dynasty in China, a Japanese monk brought the first tea plant to Japan. The practice of drinking tea spread across the country - bringing forth different preparations that tea variations - like matcha and sencha green tea.

The introduction of tea to Japan also created a new cultural practice through Japanese tea ceremonies. It became a staple practice in Japanese culture due to its significance in building their traditional values of hospitality and careful handling and presentation of tea.

Expansion to the West

As China became the leading export of tea (along with silk and porcelain), the Dutch traders discovered the existence of tea in China. They began to spread the influence of tea in Europe by bringing it in large quantities through trading in different countries - including England.

This influence began to fully shape English aristocracy and peasantry alike in Britain due to its unique taste and appeal to the people - making it a staple beverage and cultural icon in British culture and leading to several wars to monopolize the tea trade from China.

Tea in the Modern World

After numerous events, tea began to spread in different regions and was cultivated in different ways - leading to several variations. Its influence around the world also impacted the cultures and traditions of several countries - such as India, Kenya, China, Japan, and England.

With over 3,000 variations and practices worldwide, tea's identity as a staple ingredient in food and drinks in modern society only shows its significance in shaping the world's history - highlighting the historical and cultural importance of tea in people's lives.
